New to Canada Mortgages

Your foreign credit history doesn't transfer—but you're not starting from scratch. Newcomer programs offer real paths to homeownership with alternative documentation.

The Newcomer Credit Challenge

Here's what surprises most newcomers: you may have had excellent credit in your home country—a perfect payment history, multiple accounts, years of responsible borrowing—and none of it follows you to Canada.

Canadian credit bureaus (Equifax and TransUnion) don't recognize foreign credit history. You arrive with a blank slate, regardless of your financial track record abroad. For mortgage purposes, you're essentially starting over.

The good news: lenders know this. Specific newcomer programs exist precisely because the traditional credit model doesn't work for people who just arrived. With the right documentation and the right lender, homeownership is accessible much sooner than you might think.

Your Immigration Status Determines Your Options

Status Minimum Down Payment Program Access
Permanent Resident 5% Full access to all programs
Work Permit Holder 10% Must have 183+ days validity remaining
Non-Resident / Foreign Buyer 35%+ Currently banned until Jan 1, 2027

Permanent Residents

If you have permanent resident status (PR card or Confirmation of Permanent Residence), you have access to all the same mortgage programs as Canadian citizens. This includes insured mortgages with as little as 5% down, first-time buyer incentives, and competitive rates.

The main challenge is demonstrating creditworthiness without Canadian credit history—which is where alternative documentation comes in.

Work Permit Holders

Valid work permit holders can purchase property with a minimum 10% down payment. Your permit must have at least 183 days (6 months) of validity remaining at the time of application. Some lenders require longer validity periods.

Important: if your work permit expires during your mortgage term, lenders want to see a path to renewal or PR. Employer letters confirming ongoing employment help significantly.

The Foreign Buyer Ban

Non-residents (people who are neither citizens, permanent residents, nor valid work permit holders) are currently prohibited from purchasing residential property in Canada. This ban, originally set to expire in 2025, has been extended to January 1, 2027.

There are limited exceptions for refugees, certain international students, and diplomats, but the general rule is clear: without PR or a valid work permit, you cannot purchase residential property.

Alternative Documentation: Proving Creditworthiness

Since traditional Canadian credit scores aren't available, newcomer programs accept alternative evidence of financial responsibility:

Commonly Accepted Documents

  • International credit report from your home country (if available)
  • Reference letter from foreign bank showing account history and good standing
  • 12 months of bank statements showing consistent deposits and responsible management
  • Rental payment history (cancelled cheques, bank records, or landlord letter)
  • Utility payment history (phone, hydro, internet bills)
  • Employment letter confirming position, salary, and tenure
  • Foreign tax returns or income documentation

Tip: Start gathering these documents before you apply. Getting a reference letter from a foreign bank can take weeks. The more alternative documentation you can provide, the stronger your application.

Down Payment Requirements

Newcomer programs typically follow standard Canadian down payment rules:

Purchase Price Minimum Down Payment
Up to $500,000 5% (PR) or 10% (Work Permit)
$500,001 - $1,500,000 5% on first $500K + 10% on remainder
Over $1,500,000 20%

Down Payment Sources

Lenders need to verify where your down payment comes from. Acceptable sources include:

  • Personal savings (90-day bank statement history required)
  • Gift from immediate family (gift letter required)
  • Funds transferred from abroad (foreign exchange records, wire transfer documentation)
  • Sale of property abroad (sale documents, proof of ownership)

Important: If transferring funds from abroad, document the paper trail thoroughly. Lenders and lawyers need to see the source of funds, the transfer, and the arrival in your Canadian account. Large unexplained deposits raise red flags.

Non-Resident Speculation Tax (NRST)

Ontario charges a 25% Non-Resident Speculation Tax on residential property purchases by foreign nationals and foreign corporations. As of January 2025, Toronto added an additional 10% municipal NRST, bringing the total to 35% in Toronto.

However, this tax does NOT apply to:

  • Canadian citizens
  • Permanent residents
  • Protected persons (refugees)
  • Valid work permit holders (in most cases)

If you're a PR or work permit holder, you should be exempt from NRST. Your lawyer will verify your status and ensure the exemption is properly claimed at closing.

Lender Programs for Newcomers

Most major lenders have specific newcomer programs. While details vary, common features include:

Time-in-Canada requirements: Some programs require you to have been in Canada for a minimum period (often 3-6 months), while others have no minimum.

Employment requirements: Typically need to be employed in Canada with verifiable income. Some programs accept job offers if you're newly arrived.

Maximum amortization: Usually 25 years for newcomer programs (30-year amortizations may require established Canadian credit).

Property restrictions: Some programs limit you to owner-occupied properties only (no investment properties until you have Canadian credit history).

Why a Broker Matters More for Newcomers

Each lender's newcomer program has different criteria, documentation requirements, and flexibility. A broker knows which lenders work best for specific situations:

  • Which have the fastest approval timelines

Walking into a single bank means one set of criteria. Working with a broker means accessing 30+ lenders and finding the one that fits your specific profile.

Building Canadian Credit

Even if you don't need Canadian credit for your first mortgage, building it early helps with future refinancing, renewals, and other financial products.

Immediate steps:

  • Get a secured credit card—you deposit funds as collateral, then use it like a regular card. Neo, Refresh, and others offer these.
  • Get a phone plan in your name—some providers report to credit bureaus.
  • Put utilities in your name—consistent payment builds history.
  • Consider a credit builder loan—Borrowell and KOHO offer products specifically designed to build credit.

With consistent on-time payments, you can establish a usable Canadian credit score within 6-12 months.

Your Next Steps

  1. Confirm your immigration status—PR, work permit validity, or path to PR
  2. Gather alternative documentation—foreign bank letters, credit reports, rental history
  3. Document your down payment source—90 days of bank statements, transfer records
  4. Get employment verification—employer letter confirming position and salary
  5. Talk to a broker—we'll identify which newcomer programs fit your situation

Being new to Canada doesn't mean waiting years to buy a home. With the right documentation and the right lender, homeownership is often accessible much sooner than newcomers expect.
