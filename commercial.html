Commercial Mortgage Lending

Once you cross five units, the rules change completely. Commercial lending is about cash flow, cap rates, and the property's ability to service debt—not your personal income.

When Residential Becomes Commercial

The magic number is five. A property with 1-4 residential units qualifies for residential mortgage financing. Five units or more, and you're in commercial territory—different lenders, different qualification methods, different terms.

This transition trips up many investors. You can't simply scale a residential mortgage strategy to a 6-plex or 20-unit building. The entire underwriting approach changes.

What Counts as Commercial?

  • Multi-residential: 5+ unit apartment buildings
  • Mixed-use: Residential units above commercial retail/office
  • Retail: Strip malls, standalone stores
  • Office: Professional buildings, office complexes
  • Industrial: Warehouses, manufacturing facilities
  • Special purpose: Hotels, self-storage, gas stations, etc.

How Commercial Loans Are Underwritten

Residential mortgages focus on you—your income, your credit, your debt ratios. Commercial mortgages focus on the property—its income, its expenses, its ability to service debt.

Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR)

The most important metric in commercial lending. DSCR measures whether the property's net operating income (NOI) can cover the mortgage payments.

DSCR = Net Operating Income ÷ Annual Debt Service

Example: Property generates $120,000 NOI. Annual mortgage payments are $100,000.
DSCR = $120,000 ÷ $100,000 = 1.20

Most lenders require a minimum DSCR of 1.20 to 1.30—meaning the property must generate 20-30% more income than needed to cover the mortgage. This provides a cushion for vacancies, unexpected expenses, or income fluctuations.

Net Operating Income (NOI)

NOI is gross rental income minus operating expenses (before mortgage payments). Lenders scrutinize these numbers carefully:

  • Gross potential income: What the property would generate at 100% occupancy
  • Vacancy allowance: Typically 3-5% deducted even if fully occupied
  • Operating expenses: Property taxes, insurance, utilities (if paid by landlord), maintenance, management fees, reserves

The resulting NOI determines how much debt the property can support.

Capitalization Rate (Cap Rate)

Cap rate measures the relationship between NOI and property value:

Cap Rate = NOI ÷ Property Value

Lower cap rates indicate higher prices relative to income (typically in prime locations). Higher cap rates suggest higher returns but often more risk or less desirable locations.

Property Type Typical Cap Rate Range
Multi-residential (major city) 4.0% - 5.5%
Multi-residential (secondary market) 5.5% - 7.5%
Retail (good location) 5.5% - 7.0%
Office 6.0% - 8.0%
Industrial 5.0% - 7.0%

Commercial Mortgage Terms

Commercial mortgages differ from residential in several important ways:

Feature Residential Commercial
Down payment 5-20% 25-35% typical
Amortization 25-30 years 20-25 years typical
Term 1-10 years 1-10 years
Interest rates Prime or bond + spread Higher spread over benchmarks
Prepayment Varies, often flexible Often restricted or penalized
Recourse Full recourse May be limited or non-recourse

Rate Structures

Commercial rates are typically quoted as a spread over a benchmark:

  • Government of Canada bonds + spread (e.g., 5-year GoC + 2.00%)
  • Prime rate + spread for variable options
  • CMHC-insured multi-family gets preferential rates (more on this below)

CMHC Multi-Unit Mortgage Insurance

Here's something many investors don't know: CMHC insures multi-unit residential properties (5+ units), and this insurance unlocks significantly better terms.

Benefits of CMHC-Insured Commercial

  • Lower rates: Often 0.50-1.00% below conventional commercial
  • Higher LTV: Up to 85% (vs. 65-75% conventional)
  • Longer amortization: Up to 40 years (vs. 20-25 conventional)
  • Better cash flow: Lower payments improve DSCR

Requirements

  • Minimum 5 self-contained residential units
  • Located in a market with demonstrated rental demand
  • Net worth requirement (typically 25% of loan amount)
  • Property condition standards
  • DSCR minimums (typically 1.10 with insurance vs. 1.20+ without)

Example impact: On a $2M apartment building, CMHC insurance could reduce the down payment from $600K (70% LTV) to $300K (85% LTV) while also lowering the interest rate. That's $300K freed up for your next acquisition.

Types of Commercial Lenders

Schedule I Banks (Big 5)

Conservative underwriting, competitive rates for strong deals, relationship-focused. Best for experienced investors with strong financials and stabilized properties.

Credit Unions

Often more flexible than banks, particularly for smaller deals or local properties. May have geographic restrictions.

Life Insurance Companies

Long-term outlook, competitive rates, prefer larger deals ($5M+). Less flexible but very stable.

CMHC-Approved Lenders

Specialize in multi-unit residential. Access to CMHC insurance programs for qualifying properties.

Private/Alternative Lenders

Higher rates but more flexible criteria. Useful for transitional situations, value-add plays, or when traditional lenders decline.

Documentation Requirements

Commercial lenders want extensive documentation on the property:

  • Rent roll: Current tenants, unit sizes, lease terms, rental rates
  • Lease abstracts: Summaries of all lease agreements
  • Operating statements: 2-3 years of income and expense history
  • Property tax bills: Current and historical
  • Insurance certificates: Current coverage
  • Environmental reports: Phase I ESA often required
  • Appraisal: Commercial appraisal (more detailed than residential)
  • Building condition report: For older properties

On the borrower side:

  • Net worth statement: Personal assets and liabilities
  • Real estate schedule: All properties owned
  • Corporate documents: If purchasing through a corporation
  • Tax returns: 2-3 years personal and/or corporate

Commercial Deal Timeline

Commercial transactions take longer than residential:

Stage Timeline
Initial lender review 1-2 weeks
Letter of Intent / Term Sheet 1-2 weeks
Due diligence / underwriting 4-8 weeks
Commitment / approval 1-2 weeks
Legal / closing 2-4 weeks

Total: 8-16 weeks is typical for commercial transactions. Plan accordingly when making offers.

Your Next Steps

  1. Gather property financials—rent roll, operating statements, tax bills
  2. Calculate the NOI—understand the property's true cash flow
  3. Prepare your net worth statement—lenders will want to see your overall financial picture
  4. Consider CMHC eligibility—if it's 5+ residential units, the benefits are significant
  5. Talk to a broker—commercial lending requires matching the right lender to the specific deal

Commercial real estate financing is more complex than residential, but the right structure can make deals work that wouldn't otherwise pencil. It's worth getting expert guidance from the start.
