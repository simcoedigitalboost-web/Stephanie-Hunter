Mortgage Renewals & Switches

Your renewal letter arrives. You sign it. You've just left thousands of dollars on the table. Let's fix that.

The Renewal Letter Trap

About 120 days before your mortgage term ends, you'll receive a renewal letter from your current lender. It'll have a rate on it. Maybe a few options. The letter makes it easy: just sign here, and you're done for another term.

60% of Canadians sign that letter without shopping around.

Here's what they don't realize: that renewal letter is a starting negotiation, not a final offer. And often, better rates exist at other lenders—rates your current lender isn't offering because they're counting on inertia.

Average savings from switching lenders at renewal: $13,857 over a 5-year term. That's not a typo. The difference between passively accepting a renewal and actively shopping can be substantial.

The 2025 Renewal Wave

We're in the middle of the largest mortgage renewal cycle in Canadian history. 1.2 million mortgages ($352 billion) are renewing in 2025 alone. Many of these borrowers locked in at historic lows—rates around 1.5-2.5%—and are now facing rates more than double that.

If that's you, the rate you secured in 2020-2022 simply doesn't exist anymore. But that doesn't mean you should accept the first offer your lender sends. The spread between what banks offer in renewal letters and what's available through competitive shopping is often 0.25-0.75%—and that gap translates to real money.

The Stress Test Change That Benefits Renewers

Here's important news if you're considering switching lenders: as of November 2024, OSFI eliminated the stress test for uninsured mortgage switches at renewal.

Previously, if you wanted to switch to a new lender at renewal, you had to re-qualify at the stress test rate (your rate + 2% or 5.25%, whichever is higher)—even if you'd been making payments flawlessly for years. This trapped many borrowers with their current lender.

Now, if your mortgage is uninsured (you put 20%+ down originally), you can switch lenders at renewal without re-qualifying under the stress test. This opens up competitive options that were previously blocked.

Note: This applies to switches only—same mortgage amount, same amortization remaining. If you want to refinance (increase your mortgage, extend amortization, or access equity), the stress test still applies.

Renewal vs. Switch: What's the Difference?

Renewal (Stay) Switch (Move)
What happens Sign new term with current lender Transfer mortgage to new lender
Stress test Not required Not required (as of Nov 2024)*
Appraisal Usually not required May be required
Legal fees None Often covered by new lender
Timeline Simple—sign and done 2-4 weeks typical
Penalty None at maturity None at maturity

*For uninsured mortgages with no increase to principal or amortization

The Big Bank Penalty Trap

If you're mid-term and considering breaking your mortgage early, pay attention to how your lender calculates penalties. This is where Big 5 banks often cost borrowers dearly.

There are two penalty calculations:

  • 3 months' interest: Straightforward—3 months of interest at your current rate
  • Interest Rate Differential (IRD): Compensation for the rate difference over your remaining term

Your penalty is whichever is higher. The problem? Big banks use posted rates (not actual discounted rates) in their IRD calculations, dramatically inflating the penalty.

Scenario Big 5 Bank Penalty Monoline Lender Penalty
$400K mortgage, 3 years remaining, 1.5% rate drop $14,000+ $2,600 - $4,000

Same mortgage. Same situation. Penalty difference of $10,000+ purely based on how the lender calculates IRD.

This matters at renewal because choosing the right lender now protects you if circumstances change mid-term. Life happens—job relocations, divorces, unexpected needs for equity. Monoline lenders (First National, MCAP, RMG, etc.) typically use fairer penalty calculations than the Big 5.

When to Start Shopping

120 days before maturity: Most lenders allow rate holds 120 days out. This is when you should start the conversation—not when you receive the renewal letter.

90-120 days: Ideal window. You have time to shop, compare, and make a decision without rushing.

30-60 days: Still workable, but options may be limited and timelines tight.

Under 30 days: Possible but stressful. Some lenders can't process switches this quickly.

Don't let your mortgage go past maturity without a plan. If your term expires and you haven't renewed or switched, most lenders will automatically put you on their posted variable rate—often significantly higher than market rates.

Beyond Rate: What Else Matters at Renewal

Rate is important, but it's not everything. Consider:

Prepayment privileges: Can you make lump sum payments? Increase your regular payments? 15/15 (15% lump sum, 15% payment increase) is standard; some lenders offer 20/20 or better.

Portability: If you sell and buy within a certain window, can you transfer your mortgage without penalty? Important if you might move.

Penalty calculation: As discussed, this varies dramatically. Fair IRD calculation can save you thousands if life changes.

Blend-and-extend options: If rates drop mid-term, can you blend your current rate with a new rate and extend?

Collateral charge vs. standard charge: Some lenders register as collateral charges (TD, Tangerine, National Bank), making switches more expensive. Standard charges transfer more easily.

Your Next Steps

  1. Know your maturity date—mark 120 days before in your calendar
  2. Find your current mortgage statement—know your balance, rate, and remaining amortization
  3. Don't sign the renewal letter until you've shopped
  4. Talk to a broker—we'll show you what's actually available

Shopping your renewal takes minimal effort—a conversation, some paperwork, and a couple of weeks. The potential savings justify the time many times over.
