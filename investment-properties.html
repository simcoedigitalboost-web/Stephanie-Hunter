Investment Property Mortgages

Different rules, different lenders, different strategies. From your first rental to building a portfolio, here's what you need to know about financing investment properties.

Investment Properties Play by Different Rules

Financing an investment property isn't the same as buying a home you'll live in. Down payment requirements are higher, rates carry a premium, and how lenders calculate rental income varies dramatically from one institution to another.

That last point—rental income calculation—is where most investors either maximize or leave significant purchasing power on the table. The difference between lenders can mean $50,000 to $100,000 in additional qualification.

Down Payment: 20% Minimum, No Exceptions

Investment properties cannot be insured by CMHC, Sagen, or Canada Guaranty. That means no high-ratio mortgages—you need at least 20% down, period.

Property Type Minimum Down Payment
Single-family rental 20%
Duplex (non-owner occupied) 20%
Triplex/Fourplex (non-owner occupied) 20%
5+ units Commercial lending (see Commercial)

Owner-occupied exception: If you'll live in one unit of a 2-4 unit property, you can put as little as 5-10% down (treated as owner-occupied). Many investors start this way—live in one unit, rent the others, then move out and repeat.

Interest Rate Premiums

Expect to pay 0.25% to 0.50% more than owner-occupied rates for investment properties. This premium reflects higher perceived risk—investors are statistically more likely to default under financial stress than people living in their homes.

How Lenders Calculate Rental Income

This is where lender selection matters most. There are two primary methods for calculating how rental income affects your qualification:

Method 1: Rental Offset

The lender adds a portion of rental income (typically 50-80%) to offset the property's carrying costs. Any remainder helps your debt service ratios, but it's primarily used to cover the rental property's own mortgage payment.

Method 2: Add-Back (More Favorable)

The lender adds rental income directly to your gross income for qualification purposes. This can dramatically increase your borrowing power because the rental income helps qualify you for more mortgage across your entire portfolio—not just offset one property.

The difference is massive. On a property with $2,500/month rental income, the add-back method can provide $50,000 to $100,000 more in purchasing power compared to offset-only calculation. Choosing the right lender is critical.

Rental Income Documentation

For existing rentals, lenders want to see:

  • Current lease agreements
  • T776 (Statement of Real Estate Rentals) from your tax return
  • Proof of rent deposits (bank statements)

For properties you're purchasing, lenders typically use an appraisal's market rent estimate or the existing lease if tenants are in place.

Portfolio Limits: When Banks Say No

Most major banks cap their exposure to any single real estate investor:

  • 4-5 properties is a common limit at Big 5 banks
  • $1.5M to $2M total exposure including your principal residence
  • Some banks count number of mortgages, others count total debt

Once you hit these limits, you need to move to lenders who specialize in portfolio investors—typically B-lenders, credit unions, or portfolio-specific programs.

OSFI's 2026 IPRRE Rule

A significant regulatory change is coming: starting in 2026, OSFI will classify borrowers whose rental income exceeds 50% of their qualifying income as "Income-Producing Real Estate" (IPRRE) borrowers. This classification triggers additional capital requirements for lenders, which may result in:

  • Higher rates for heavy portfolio investors
  • More stringent qualification requirements
  • Reduced lender appetite for large portfolios

If you're building a portfolio, this rule is worth monitoring.

Financing Strategies for Investors

BRRRR (Buy, Renovate, Rent, Refinance, Repeat)

A popular strategy: buy undervalued property, renovate to increase value, rent it out, refinance to pull equity, and use that equity for the next purchase. The refinance step is critical—and requires understanding:

  • Seasoning requirements (how long before you can refinance based on new value)
  • Appraisal timing and approach
  • 80% LTV limits on refinancing

Using HELOCs for Down Payments

Many investors use a HELOC on their principal residence or existing rental for down payments on new acquisitions. This works, but remember: the HELOC payment is included in your debt service ratios when qualifying for the new mortgage.

Cross-Collateralization

Some lenders will use equity in multiple properties to secure a single loan, reducing down payment requirements on new purchases. This creates efficiency but also risk—defaulting on one property can affect others.

Tax Considerations

Investment property financing intersects with tax strategy in important ways:

Interest deductibility: Mortgage interest on investment properties is tax-deductible against rental income. This makes higher leverage potentially advantageous from a tax perspective.

Principal residence exemption: Your primary home is exempt from capital gains tax. Investment properties are not—you'll pay capital gains on the profit when you sell.

Incorporation: Some investors hold properties in corporations for liability protection and tax planning. However, many lenders won't lend to corporations at residential rates—you may need commercial financing.

Consult your accountant before making financing decisions on investment properties. The mortgage structure should align with your overall tax strategy.

Why Lender Selection Matters More for Investors

For a typical homebuyer, lender differences are primarily about rate. For investors, the differences extend to:

  • Rental income calculation method (add-back vs. offset)
  • Portfolio limits (how many properties they'll finance)
  • Debt service ratio flexibility (some extend to 50% TDS for strong files)
  • Seasoning requirements for refinancing
  • Appraisal requirements (some accept desktop appraisals)

A broker who works with investors knows which lenders fit which strategies—and can structure applications to maximize approval odds and purchasing power.

Your Next Steps

  1. Know your current portfolio position—how many properties, total debt, equity available
  2. Calculate your rental income—have lease agreements and T776 forms ready
  3. Define your strategy—buy-and-hold, BRRRR, flip, or something else?
  4. Understand your timeline—are you buying now or building toward a purchase?
  5. Talk to a broker—we'll identify which lenders and programs fit your investor profile

Investment property financing requires a more strategic approach than residential mortgages. The right lender and structure can significantly accelerate your portfolio growth—the wrong choice can stall it entirely.
