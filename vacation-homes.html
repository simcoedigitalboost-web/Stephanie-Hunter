Vacation & Second Home Mortgages

The cottage dream is alive—but financing a second property works differently than your first. From seasonal access to rental income, here's what you need to know.

Second Home vs. Investment Property

This distinction matters more than you might think. How you intend to use the property determines your financing options, down payment requirements, and interest rates.

Second Home Investment Property
Primary use Personal/family enjoyment Generating rental income
Minimum down 5% (insurable) 20% (not insurable)
Interest rate Similar to primary residence 0.25-0.50% premium
Rental income Not used for qualification Used to help qualify

If you're buying a cottage you'll use personally (even if you rent it occasionally), it typically qualifies as a second home. If the primary purpose is generating rental income through Airbnb, VRBO, or long-term tenants, it's an investment property.

The honest answer matters. Lenders will ask about intended use. Misrepresenting an investment property as a second home to get better terms is mortgage fraud. Be straightforward about your plans.

Down Payment Requirements

Second homes can be insured by CMHC, Sagen, or Canada Guaranty—which means you can put as little as 5% down on properties up to $500,000.

Purchase Price Minimum Down Payment
Up to $500,000 5%
$500,001 - $1,500,000 5% on first $500K + 10% on remainder
Over $1,500,000 20%

This is the same structure as primary residences. The key difference: you're qualifying while carrying costs for two properties, which affects your debt ratios significantly.

Qualifying While Carrying Two Properties

When you apply for a second home mortgage, lenders include your existing mortgage payment (plus property taxes, heating, and condo fees if applicable) in your debt service calculations. This "double carrying cost" is the main hurdle for second home buyers.

Debt Service Ratios

GDS (Gross Debt Service): Housing costs for both properties shouldn't exceed 39% of gross income.

TDS (Total Debt Service): All debts (both mortgages, car payments, credit cards, loans) shouldn't exceed 44% of gross income.

With two properties, these limits fill up fast. Many second home buyers have strong incomes but still bump against ratio limits once both property costs are included.

Ways to Improve Qualification

  • Pay down your primary mortgage—a lower balance means lower payments in the calculation
  • Pay off consumer debt—car loans and credit cards count against TDS
  • Larger down payment—reduces the second mortgage amount needed
  • Consider a co-borrower—adding a spouse or family member adds income to the application
  • Refinance your primary residence—extending amortization lowers the payment used in calculations

Cottage-Specific Considerations

Cottages and recreational properties come with unique challenges that can affect financing:

Seasonal vs. Year-Round Access

Properties with year-round road access are easier to finance. Seasonal-access-only properties (water access, winter roads, or roads not maintained year-round) have fewer lender options and may require larger down payments.

Some lenders won't finance seasonal-access properties at all. Others will, but with restrictions:

  • Minimum 20-35% down payment
  • Maximum LTV of 65-80%
  • Higher interest rates

Water Supply & Septic

Lenders want confirmation that the property has functional water supply and septic/sewage systems. Properties with:

  • Municipal water/sewer: No issues
  • Well water + septic: Usually fine with inspection
  • Lake water intake: Some lenders hesitant
  • Outhouse only: Very limited lender options

Construction Type

Traditional construction (wood frame, concrete foundation) finances normally. Non-standard construction may face restrictions:

  • Log homes: Many lenders okay, some decline
  • A-frames: Generally acceptable
  • Floating homes/houseboats: Very limited options
  • Tiny homes: Difficult unless on permanent foundation

Location & Remoteness

Properties in very remote locations may be declined by mainstream lenders. Factors include:

  • Distance from nearest town/services
  • Availability of emergency services
  • Comparable sales for appraisal purposes
  • Resale market liquidity

Get pre-approved before making an offer on a cottage or recreational property. The property-specific factors above can affect whether financing is available at all—better to know before you're committed.

Insurance Considerations

Lenders require property insurance, and cottage insurance has its own quirks:

Vacancy periods: Standard policies may have vacancy clauses that limit coverage if the property sits empty for extended periods (often 30+ days). Seasonal cottage policies are designed for properties that aren't occupied year-round.

Wood stove heating: If the cottage uses a wood stove as primary or secondary heat, insurers will want to see WETT (Wood Energy Technology Transfer) certification.

Distance from fire services: Properties far from fire halls may have higher premiums or require specific coverage types.

Flood zones: Waterfront properties may need separate flood insurance, which can be expensive or difficult to obtain in some areas.

The Rental Question

Many cottage owners rent their property when they're not using it. This creates a grey area:

Occasional rental (few weeks per year): Generally still qualifies as a second home. You're not using rental income to qualify, and personal use is clearly primary.

Significant rental (majority of season): Starts to look more like an investment property. If you're maximizing Airbnb bookings all summer and barely using it yourself, lenders may view it differently.

Tax implications: Rental income is taxable. If you rent your cottage, you'll report the income and can deduct a proportionate share of expenses. This also affects the principal residence exemption if you ever sell—consult an accountant.

Using Home Equity for the Down Payment

Many second home buyers tap equity in their primary residence for the down payment. Options include:

HELOC: Draw from your home equity line of credit. Remember: the HELOC payment (or interest on the drawn amount) will be included in your debt service ratios when qualifying for the cottage mortgage.

Refinance: Increase your primary mortgage and take cash out. This may make sense if you can get a better rate than your current mortgage while accessing equity.

Cross-collateralization: Some lenders will use equity in your primary residence to support the second property purchase, potentially reducing down payment requirements.

Your Next Steps

  1. Clarify your intended use—personal second home or investment property?
  2. Know your current debt situation—existing mortgage balance, car loans, credit cards
  3. Identify property characteristics—year-round access, water/septic, construction type
  4. Consider your down payment source—savings, home equity, gift?
  5. Get pre-approved early—cottage-specific factors can affect financing availability

The cottage dream is achievable, but it requires planning around the double-property carrying costs and property-specific considerations. Starting the conversation early helps identify any potential issues before you fall in love with a property that's difficult to finance.
