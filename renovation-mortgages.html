Renovation Mortgages

Roll renovation costs into your mortgage at purchase, or tap existing equity for upgrades. Multiple programs exist—here's how to choose the right one.

Ways to Finance Renovations

There are several paths to financing home improvements through your mortgage. The right choice depends on whether you're buying or already own, the scope of work, and your equity position.

Program Best For Max Amount
Purchase Plus Improvements Buyers adding renos at purchase $40,000 - $50,000
Refinance for Renovations Existing homeowners with equity Up to 80% LTV
Secondary Suite Program Adding rental units Up to 90% LTV
HELOC Flexible, ongoing access Up to 65% LTV

Purchase Plus Improvements

This program lets you roll renovation costs into your mortgage when buying a home. Instead of financing just the purchase price, you finance the purchase price plus planned improvements—all at your mortgage rate.

How It Works

  1. Get contractor quotes for the work you want done
  2. Submit quotes with your mortgage application
  3. Lender approves based on "improved value"—what the home will be worth after renovations
  4. Renovation funds held back at closing
  5. Complete work, get inspected
  6. Funds released to contractors

Limits and Requirements

  • CMHC: Up to $40,000 in improvements
  • Sagen/Canada Guaranty: Up to $50,000
  • Work must be completed within 90-120 days of closing (extensions possible)
  • Licensed contractors required for most work
  • Inspections required before funds released

Example: You're buying a home for $450,000. The kitchen needs work—contractor quote is $35,000. Your mortgage is based on $485,000 (purchase + improvements). At closing, $35,000 is held back. Once the kitchen is complete and inspected, funds are released to pay the contractor.

Eligible Improvements

Purchase Plus covers permanent improvements that add value:

  • Kitchen and bathroom renovations
  • Flooring, windows, doors
  • Electrical and plumbing upgrades
  • HVAC systems
  • Structural repairs
  • Basement finishing
  • Roof replacement
  • Accessibility modifications

Not eligible: Furniture, appliances (unless built-in), landscaping, pools, detached structures.

Why Use Purchase Plus?

Lower rate: Renovation costs financed at mortgage rates (4-5%) instead of credit card (20%+) or personal loan (8-12%) rates.

Longer amortization: Spread costs over 25-30 years instead of paying cash upfront.

Buy the "ugly" house: Homes needing work often sell below market. Purchase Plus lets you buy at a discount and finance the fix-up.

Refinancing for Renovations

If you already own your home, refinancing lets you access equity to fund improvements. You're essentially replacing your current mortgage with a larger one and taking the difference as cash.

How Much Can You Access?

Maximum refinance is 80% of your home's current value minus what you owe.

Example: Home value: $600,000. Current mortgage: $350,000.
Maximum new mortgage: $600,000 × 80% = $480,000
Available for renovations: $480,000 - $350,000 = $130,000

Costs to Consider

Refinancing isn't free. Factor in:

  • Prepayment penalty if breaking your mortgage early (can be significant)
  • Appraisal fee: $300-500
  • Legal fees: $800-1,500
  • Title insurance: $200-400

If your mortgage term is ending soon, timing the refinance at renewal avoids the prepayment penalty entirely.

The "As-Improved" Option

Some lenders will appraise based on what the home will be worth after renovations, not just current value. This can unlock more equity for larger projects. Similar to Purchase Plus, funds are held back and released as work is completed.

Secondary Suite Program (New for 2025)

As of January 2025, CMHC offers a specialized program for homeowners adding secondary suites or laneway homes. This is designed to increase housing supply by making it easier to create rental units.

Key Features

  • Up to 90% LTV—significantly higher than standard 80% refinance limit
  • 30-year amortization available
  • Can include construction costs in the mortgage
  • Projected rental income may help with qualification

Requirements

  • Must be owner-occupied property
  • Suite must be self-contained (own entrance, kitchen, bathroom)
  • Must comply with local zoning and building codes
  • Property must meet CMHC guidelines post-construction

The math can work well: If adding a basement suite costs $80,000 and generates $1,500/month rent, the rental income covers significantly more than the incremental mortgage payment—creating positive cash flow while building equity.

HELOC for Renovations

A Home Equity Line of Credit provides flexible, revolving access to your equity. You can draw funds as needed, pay down, and draw again—useful for renovations where costs emerge over time.

HELOC Basics

  • Maximum: Up to 65% LTV standalone, or up to 80% combined with a mortgage
  • Rate: Variable, typically prime + 0.5%
  • Payments: Interest-only minimum (principal optional)
  • Access: Draw and repay as needed

When HELOC Makes Sense

Phased renovations: If you're doing work over time (bathroom this year, kitchen next year), a HELOC lets you draw as needed rather than taking a lump sum upfront.

Uncertain scope: Renovations often cost more than expected. HELOC provides a buffer without having to refinance again if you go over budget.

No prepayment penalty: Unlike refinancing, setting up a HELOC doesn't require breaking your existing mortgage.

HELOC Cautions

Variable rate risk: HELOC rates move with prime. If rates rise, your carrying cost increases.

Interest-only trap: Minimum payments don't reduce principal. Discipline is required to pay down the balance.

Readvanceable mortgages: Some lenders offer products that combine a mortgage with a HELOC, automatically increasing your available credit as you pay down principal. These can be powerful tools—or dangerous if you continuously reborrow.

Choosing the Right Option

Situation Best Option
Buying a home, want to renovate immediately Purchase Plus Improvements
Own home, major renovation, want lowest rate Refinance
Adding basement suite or laneway home Secondary Suite Program
Phased renos, want flexibility HELOC
Mortgage term ending soon Refinance at renewal (no penalty)
Little equity, need major work May need personal loan or wait

Documentation Needed

For any renovation financing, expect to provide:

  • Detailed contractor quotes itemizing work and costs
  • Contractor credentials (license, insurance, WSIB)
  • Plans or drawings for significant structural work
  • Building permits if required by municipality
  • Standard mortgage documents (income verification, etc.)

Your Next Steps

  1. Define your renovation scope—what work needs to be done?
  2. Get contractor quotes—detailed, itemized estimates
  3. Know your equity position—home value minus what you owe
  4. Check your mortgage term—is renewal approaching?
  5. Talk to a broker—we'll match the right program to your situation

Renovation financing can turn a dated house into your dream home, or add income-producing space that pays for itself. The key is choosing the right tool for your specific situation.
