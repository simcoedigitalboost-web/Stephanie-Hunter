Refinancing Your Mortgage

Access your home's equity, consolidate debt, fund renovations, or restructure your mortgage for better terms. Here's how to know if refinancing makes sense.

What Refinancing Actually Means

Refinancing means replacing your current mortgage with a new one—typically for a larger amount, different rate, or different terms. Unlike a renewal or switch (where you keep the same balance), refinancing changes the fundamental structure of your mortgage.

Common reasons to refinance:

  • Access equity for renovations, investments, or major purchases
  • Consolidate high-interest debt into your lower-rate mortgage
  • Lower your interest rate if rates have dropped significantly
  • Change your amortization to adjust payment size
  • Remove someone from the title (divorce, buyout)
  • Add a secondary suite (new 90% LTV option as of January 2025)

How Much Can You Access?

The maximum loan-to-value (LTV) for a refinance is 80%—meaning you can borrow up to 80% of your home's current appraised value, minus what you owe.

Example: Your home appraises at $600,000. You owe $300,000.
Maximum new mortgage: $600,000 × 80% = $480,000
Available equity: $480,000 - $300,000 = $180,000

Exception: Secondary Suite Construction

As of January 2025, CMHC allows up to 90% LTV for refinances specifically to add a secondary suite or laneway home. This is designed to increase housing supply by making it easier to build rental units.

The Stress Test Still Applies

Unlike switches at renewal (where the stress test was recently eliminated), refinancing always requires passing the stress test. You must qualify at the higher of your contract rate plus 2%, or 5.25%.

This catches some homeowners off guard. You may have been approved for your original mortgage, but if your income has changed or you've taken on other debt, you might not qualify to refinance—even at the same payment level.

If you can't pass the stress test at an A-lender, B-lenders offer more flexibility (though at higher rates). Private lenders focus on equity rather than income qualification.

The True Cost of Refinancing

Refinancing isn't free. Before deciding, understand the full cost:

Cost Typical Range Notes
Prepayment penalty $2,000 - $15,000+ Varies by lender; IRD can be steep
Appraisal fee $300 - $500 Required to confirm home value
Legal fees $800 - $1,500 Title search, registration, discharge
Title insurance $200 - $400 Usually required
Discharge fee $200 - $400 Charged by current lender

Total typical cost: $3,500 - $17,500+ depending primarily on your prepayment penalty.

The Break-Even Calculation

Refinancing only makes sense if the benefit exceeds the cost. Here's how to think about it:

Break-even period = Total refinancing costs ÷ Monthly savings

Example: Refinancing costs $8,000. Your new payment saves $400/month.
Break-even: $8,000 ÷ $400 = 20 months
If you plan to stay in the home longer than 20 months, refinancing makes financial sense.

For debt consolidation, compare the interest you're currently paying on high-rate debt versus the blended cost of rolling it into your mortgage (including amortizing it over a longer period).

When to Refinance: Mid-Term vs. Maturity

At Maturity (No Penalty)

If your mortgage term is ending anyway, refinancing at maturity avoids the prepayment penalty entirely. This is the ideal time if you're planning to access equity or restructure.

Mid-Term (Penalty Applies)

Breaking your mortgage early means paying a penalty—either 3 months' interest or the Interest Rate Differential (IRD), whichever is higher. As discussed in the renewals section, Big 5 banks typically charge significantly higher IRD penalties than monoline lenders.

60% of Canadians break their mortgage before the term ends. The median is just 38 months into a 5-year term. Life happens—and when it does, penalty calculation matters enormously.

When Mid-Term Refinancing Still Makes Sense

  • Debt consolidation where interest savings dwarf the penalty
  • Rate drop significant enough to justify costs
  • Life changes requiring immediate equity access
  • Investment opportunity with strong expected returns

Refinance Rates vs. Purchase Rates

Refinance rates are typically 0.10% to 0.25% higher than purchase or renewal rates. This premium reflects the additional risk and work involved for lenders.

Term Purchase/Renewal Rate Refinance Rate
5-year fixed 3.79% - 4.29% 3.89% - 4.50%
3-year fixed 3.59% - 4.09% 3.69% - 4.25%
Variable Prime - 0.90% Prime - 0.70%

Alternatives to Refinancing

Depending on your goal, refinancing may not be the best option:

HELOC (Home Equity Line of Credit): Access equity without breaking your mortgage. Revolving credit at variable rates (typically prime + 0.5%). Can often be set up as a second position behind your existing mortgage. Good for ongoing access to funds rather than a lump sum.

Second Mortgage: A separate loan secured against your home's equity. Higher rates than a refinance (often 7-15%), but no penalty on your first mortgage. Works when refinancing isn't feasible.

Blend-and-Extend: Some lenders let you blend your current rate with a new rate and extend your term. Avoids the full penalty while accessing some equity or getting a better rate.

Wait for Maturity: If your term ends within 12-18 months and the need isn't urgent, waiting may save thousands in penalties.

The Refinancing Process

  1. Consultation: We review your goals, current mortgage, and calculate whether refinancing makes sense
  2. Application: Submit income documentation, consent for credit check
  3. Appraisal: Lender orders appraisal to confirm property value
  4. Approval: Lender issues commitment based on appraisal and qualification
  5. Legal: Lawyer handles discharge of old mortgage, registration of new one
  6. Funding: New mortgage funds, old mortgage paid out, equity distributed

Timeline: 2-4 weeks typical from application to funding, though complex situations may take longer.

Your Next Steps

  1. Know your current mortgage details—balance, rate, maturity date, lender
  2. Estimate your home's current value—recent sales in your neighborhood give a rough idea
  3. Calculate your potential equity—80% of value minus what you owe
  4. Define your goal—what will you do with the funds?
  5. Talk to a broker—we'll run the break-even analysis and show you whether it makes sense

Refinancing can be a powerful financial tool when used strategically. It can also be an expensive mistake if the numbers don't work. A proper analysis before committing ensures you make the right call.
